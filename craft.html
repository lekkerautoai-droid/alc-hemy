The Craft

To make something worth drinking, you must first understand everything it must overcome.

I

Step One

Forage
✦

Every season, before the lab opens its doors, before the fire under the copper pot is lit, we go out into the land. Not because we must — we could order everything from a catalogue — but because the finest ingredients are not the most expensive ones. They are the most present ones.

Wild thyme gathered from a chalk hillside at dawn has a sharpness that no dried extract can reproduce. Elderflower picked at the moment the blossom opens carries a green, almost electric fragrance. Blackthorn sloe, harvested after the first frost, contains a tartness that the bottle version cannot manufacture.

We maintain relationships with three farms within forty miles. We forage personally from two designated woodlands under conservation management. What we cannot grow or gather, we source from producers whose methods we can stand behind — which means the list is short, and that is as it should be.

  • Wild botanicals gathered across four seasons — no single harvest is replicated
  • Partnerships with Ferndale Farm (herbs), Ashwood Orchard (fruit), and Brackwater Apiaries (raw honey)
  • Zero synthetic flavourings in any ALC_Hemy product — ever
  • Each forage log is kept: date, location, weather, notes — the archaeology of a drink
II

Step Two

Ferment
✦

Patience is not a virtue in fermentation — it is a technique. We work with open-crock, cold-temperature fermentation in sealed stone vessels that were used in this barn before we arrived. The cultures within them are older than our business.

We ferment honey, fruit, grain, and occasionally things that have no category yet — a kind of experimental alchemy that sits at the edge of the process and sometimes produces failures so interesting that we study them before discarding them.

Our house shrubs — the vinegar-based syrups that give many of our cocktails their edge — are fermented for a minimum of three weeks. Our wild-yeast fermented honey takes six. Time is the most expensive ingredient on our shelf, and we have never tried to negotiate with it.

  • Open-crock cold ferments in 18th-century stone vessels — cultures maintained continuously
  • Wild yeast strains cultivated from apple orchards and meadow flowers
  • House shrubs: aged minimum three weeks in ceramic-lined vessels
  • Fermentation logs dating back to our founding in 2019 — every batch is numbered
III

Step Three

Distil
✦

Our copper pot still has a name. We call her Patience, because that is what she demands and what she rewards. She is a hand-hammered 150-litre copper pot still acquired from a decommissioned Highland distillery in 2021, and she has been the heart of this operation ever since.

Distillation is a conversation. The still speaks in temperature, in vapour pressure, in the subtle colour of the condensate. Our lead alchemist runs every distillation personally, taking cuts by nose and taste — not by instrument. The head runs fast and harsh and is discarded. The heart runs slow, clear, and honest. The tail we watch carefully for the moments when it still has something to say.

We do not aim for efficiency. We aim for character. The hearts cut from Patience represent perhaps 50–60% of what the wash produces. The rest is composted, re-distilled for cleaning purposes, or saved for the students at our Bitters Lab.

  • 150-litre hand-hammered copper pot still — acquired 2021, decommissioned Highland origin
  • All cuts made by nose and taste — no automated monitoring during the heart run
  • Hearts yield: approximately 55% of wash — slow, deliberate, character-first
  • All distillation performed by our lead mixologist, Nicolas Kruger — never delegated
IV

Step Four

Compose
✦

The final act. After forage, fermentation, and distillation have each played their part, composition is where we stop being craftspeople and begin being artists. A cocktail is a sentence. Every element earns its place — or it leaves.

We test every new composition a minimum of twelve times before it appears on the menu. We taste it at different temperatures, at different times of day, with different foods and moods in mind. We invite four regular guests to blind-taste at every stage of development — people who will tell us the honest truth because they have learned that we prefer it.

Composition also means restraint. Our longest menu has been fourteen cocktails. We have served as few as seven. We do not add a drink because we need volume — we add it when it earns its place in the sequence of the evening. A menu is a programme, not a catalogue.

  • Minimum twelve development iterations before any cocktail reaches the menu
  • Blind-tasting panel of four regular guests — feedback unfiltered and non-negotiable
  • Menu compositions reviewed with each seasonal forage — nothing stays for sentiment alone
  • Every final recipe is written by hand into the ALC_Hemy Codex — our living recipe book

The Still

Meet Patience.

✦ ✦ ✦

She was decommissioned from a family distillery in Speyside when the family decided — reluctantly, after three generations — that a copper pot still could not survive the economics of modern spirits production. They were right about the economics. They were wrong about the still.

Patience arrived in our barn on a grey November morning in 2021, transported on a flatbed, wrapped in old cotton sheeting that still faintly smelled of single malt. We spent three weeks cleaning, re-sealing, and relearning what she required before we ran a single distillation.

She is opinionated. She runs warmer on the left side and colder through the worm at certain barometric pressures. She requires a specific pace of heat ramp-up, or she sulks. In return, she produces a distillate of extraordinary purity and — despite everything — patience.

Material

The Larder

Our Ingredients

☕
Wild Botanicals

Gathered personally from designated woodlands. Thyme, verbena, heather, clover, pine needle, yarrow. The wild keeps a different calendar.

⚘
Raw Honey

From Brackwater Apiaries — unfiltered, unheated, tasting entirely of the hedgerow miles the bees have covered. Used in syrups, ferments, and glazes.

△
Seasonal Fruit

Orchard fruit from Ashwood — damson, quince, pear, greengage. Pressed cold, reduced slowly, or fermented whole. Never compromised by out-of-season supply.

◆
Heritage Grains

Malted barley, heritage rye, and emmer wheat from a single farm in Dorset. Ground on site. The bread of the distillery — and sometimes the distillery of the bread.

✦ ✦ ✦

We believe that provenance is not a marketing position — it is an ethical obligation. Every ingredient in an ALC_Hemy cocktail has a name: the farm, the field, the person who tended it. We keep these names in the Codex and we are happy to share them on request.

We do not use artificial flavourings, synthetic colours, or pre-made syrups. If we cannot make it ourselves or source it from someone whose methods we respect, it does not appear in a glass.

The Alchemists

The people behind the pour.

Three people run ALC_Hemy. That number is deliberate. It is enough to keep the standard, not so many that accountability dilutes.

Lead Mixologist · Founder
Nicolas Kruger

Born in Cape Town, forged behind bars across three continents. Nicolas founded ALC_Hemy on the conviction that every great cocktail is an act of alchemy — patient, precise, and impossible to rush. He brought the obsession home, and built the lab around it.

Speciality: Botanical Distillation · Bitters Architecture
Head of Composition
Maren Solberg

Formerly a pastry chef in Oslo. Maren crossed into cocktails because, as she puts it, the bar has better physics. She is responsible for every menu composition at ALC_Hemy and approaches each one as a small work of architecture. She will tell you when something is wrong before you have tasted it.

Speciality: Seasonal Menu Design · Fermentation
Cellarman · Forager
Theo Crane

Theo came to ALC_Hemy via six years running a hedgerow foraging company and a brief, ill-fated stint as a whisky auctioneer. He tends the cellar, manages all sourcing relationships, and is responsible for the foraging programme. He knows exactly what grows within twenty miles and when to pick it.

Speciality: Foraging · Cellar · Barrel Ageing
